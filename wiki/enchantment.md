# Enchantment

**Summary**: Guy Kawasaki's framework for winning people over. You change hearts, minds, and actions through quality, likability, and trustworthiness — not pressure or persuasion.

**Sources**: Guy Kawasaki, "Enchantment". Personal practice.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-26

---

Enchantment is about changing people's hearts, minds, and actions — not by convincing or pushing, but by being genuinely good, likable, and trustworthy.

Kawasaki's core argument: the way to get people on your side is not to win arguments. It is to be someone worth following. That requires:

- **Quality** — do excellent work. It speaks before you do.
- **Likability** — be someone people want to work with. Smile. Be present. Be interested.
- **Trustworthiness** — do what you say. Be honest about what you don't know.

## Why I teach this

In software teams, people have a lot of implicit influence — over technical direction, over culture, over how work gets done. That influence is rarely formal. It accumulates through reputation and relationship.

I have seen technically brilliant developers with almost no influence because nobody wanted to work with them. And I have seen developers with modest technical skills who shaped teams because people trusted and liked them.

Enchantment is the long game. It is also the right game.

## Connection to the rest of the philosophy

Kawasaki's "you win *with* people" maps directly to the meta-theme of [[software-is-a-human-discipline]]. [[nonviolent-communication]] gives you the tools to communicate in a way that builds trust. [[responsibility-process]] keeps you above the line — blaming and justifying are not enchanting.
