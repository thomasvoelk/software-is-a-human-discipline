# Curiosity Over Anger

**Summary**: When someone has a different opinion and you feel anger or defensiveness rising, flip the switch: go into curiosity mode.

**Sources**: J.B. Rainsberger (tweet). Daniel Goleman, "Emotional Intelligence" (1995). Personal experience and practice.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-26

---

Earlier in my career I had a real problem. When someone had a different opinion than me — a code review, a design discussion with an architect — and I was convinced I was right, my body reacted. Elevated heartbeat. A glass of water shaking in my hand.

Then I read a tweet by J.B. Rainsberger. The idea was simple: when someone has a different opinion and you feel anger rising, flip the switch and go into curiosity mode.

*"I have never thought about this before. Tell me more."* — or if you have: *"That sounds interesting. Tell me more."*

It took serious practice. But the anger is gone. I am now ultra relaxed in exactly the situations that used to destabilize me.

## The physical signal is the cue

The shaking hand, the tight throat, the warm face — these are not enemies. They are signals. They tell you: you are about to react from a powerless place. That is when to flip. See [[the-switch]].

A few months ago a manager told me: "Thomas, you sometimes ask me questions in meetings where my throat feels tight and I feel warm." I recognized immediately what she was describing — I had lived it. I explained the trick. The symptoms she described are not weakness; they are a deeply rooted biological response. Daniel Goleman calls it the "amygdala hijack" (*Emotional Intelligence*, 1995): a perceived threat — including a social one like disagreement or criticism — triggers the same fight-or-flight mechanism as a physical danger. Heart rate rises, muscles tighten, face flushes. Evolution, not character. The signal that it is time to flip.

## Why this matters in software

This shows up everywhere: code reviews, architecture discussions, requirements meetings with customers, retrospectives. Someone challenges your idea and your brain reads it as an attack on your identity. That reaction — however understandable — gets in the way. In my experience, curiosity produces better outcomes than winning.

## Connection to NVC

Flipping the switch does not just stop the bad reaction — it opens the door to something more. Once you are no longer in fight-or-flight, you can actually listen. Rosenberg calls this *giving empathy* — listening for the other person's feelings and needs rather than just their words. [[nonviolent-communication]] gives you the full framework for that.
