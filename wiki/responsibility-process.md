# Responsibility Process

**Summary**: Christopher Avery's model of how people respond to problems. Most responses are powerless. Only one is not.

**Sources**: Christopher Avery, "The Responsibility Process" (https://responsibility.com/responsibility-process/, https://responsibility.com/exploring-the-stages/). German overview: https://www.it-agile.de/agiles-wissen/agile-leadership/was-ist-the-responsibility-processtm/. Personal experience and practice.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-26

---

Avery defines a problem simply: "If I don't have what I want, or if I have something I don't want, I have a problem." The process is triggered by the anxiety or anger that signals this gap.

When something goes wrong, most people move through a predictable sequence of powerless states before — if they ever get there — arriving at genuine responsibility.

Avery's ladder, from bottom to top:

1. **Denial** — "There is no problem."
2. **Blame** — "It's their fault."
3. **Justify** — "Yes, but there were reasons."
4. **Shame** — "I'm terrible."
5. **Obligation** — "I have to fix this." (still powerless — driven by external pressure)
6. **Quit** — escape as a coping mechanism
7. **Responsibility** — owning the ability and power to create, choose, and attract. "I create my experience. What will I do?"

Only Responsibility is powerful. Everything below it is a form of avoidance.

## How I use this

This is the first framework I show people when I mentor them, because it changes how you read every conversation and every conflict. Once you see the ladder, you start noticing where you are — and where others are — in real time.

The practical move is [[the-switch]]: whenever I catch myself below the line — blaming a stakeholder, justifying a bad decision, feeling ashamed of a mistake — I name the state and flip to: *I create my experience. What can I do?*

This pairs directly with Seth Godin's "it is always your turn" — see [[it-is-always-your-turn]]. If you are at Responsibility, there is always a move available to you.

## Why I teach this first

Avery's point is that this is not a character flaw — it is a mental process operating identically in everyone. It is the starting condition. Showing junior developers this model early gives them the chance to recognise these states in themselves and move to where the real power is: the feeling that you are bigger than the problem, that you have the ability to see and resolve it. And that is an amazing feeling.
