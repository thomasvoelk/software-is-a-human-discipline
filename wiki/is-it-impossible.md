# Is it impossible, or do I just not know how?

**Summary**: "This doesn't work" and "I can't do this" often mean "I don't know how to do this yet." The instinct to quit a new practice early is almost always premature.

**Sources**: J.B. Rainsberger. Ron Jeffries, "We Tried Baseball and It Didn't Work" (https://ronjeffries.com/xprog/articles/jatbaseball/). Personal experience.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-26

---

When I try something new and it does not click immediately, the instinct is to declare it broken. *This sucks. This doesn't work. This is not for me.*

J.B. Rainsberger made an observation that stuck with me: when someone says "this is not possible" or "this doesn't work", they often mean "I don't know how to make it work." Those are very different statements. One closes the door. The other opens it.

Ron Jeffries illustrates the other failure mode in his baseball article: the person who takes a practice, quietly changes the rules to suit themselves, watches it fall apart, and then announces it doesn't work. The problem was never the practice.

## The outside switch

I have a teammate who catches me before I quit. When I say "this sucks", he asks: maybe you are not doing it right? Maybe you don't know enough yet? Maybe you need more practice?

That used to be an outside switch — I needed him to flip it for me. Two examples where his nudging changed everything for me and our whole team:

- Documenting in Markdown in a GitHub repo instead of Confluence. I was not convinced. We tried it properly. It was better.
- Automating GitHub repo setups with Terraform. Same resistance. Same result.

In both cases my initial reaction was wrong. The practice was not the problem. I was the problem — I just did not know it well enough yet.

## The self-switch

I am developing this as a self-switch now. When I feel the "this sucks" reaction, I try to catch it and ask the question: *is it actually impossible, or do I just not know how?*

It is not yet as automatic as the [[curiosity-over-anger]] switch or the [[responsibility-process]] switch. But I know what it looks like when I do it right, because I have seen it from the outside.
