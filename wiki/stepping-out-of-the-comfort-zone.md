# Stepping Out of the Comfort Zone

**Summary**: Common advice, but underpowered on its own. Rilke and Avery together explain why it works — and what inner posture makes the discomfort productive rather than just painful.

**Sources**: Rainer Maria Rilke, "Der Schauende" (Das Buch der Bilder, 1902). Christopher Avery, "The Responsibility Process" (https://responsibility.com/exploring-the-stages/). Personal experience.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-27

---

"Step out of your comfort zone" is good advice. But it is thin. It tells you what to do without explaining why it works or what you need to bring to it.

## What Rilke adds

Rilke's poem "Der Schauende" ("The Man Watching") uses the story of Jacob wrestling the angel to make a sharper point than the comfort zone framing usually does:

> *Sein Wachstum ist: der Tiefbesiegte*
> *von immer Größerem zu sein.*

"His growth is: to be deeply defeated by ever greater beings."

The implication is that if you only seek challenges you can already handle, you plateau. You get more competent within a range you already control, but you don't expand it. Growth happens at the edge where things genuinely exceed you — not in the middle where you are comfortable, and not just one step beyond it either.

## What Avery adds

Knowing you should seek greater challenges is not enough. You also need the right inner posture when you face them.

Avery's [[responsibility-process]] describes the only posture that works: the conviction that you are bigger than the problem, that you have the power and ability to see and resolve it. The states below that line — blame, shame, obligation — are all forms of retreating from the encounter. You can be pushed into something hard and still gain nothing from it, if your response is simply to endure it passively until it is over.

Stepping out of your comfort zone works when you bring Responsibility to it. And that is much easier to do when the choice is yours.

## A personal story

Early in my career, my boss Chris Rupp assigned me to present at a conference. When I objected that I had never presented anything before, she said simply: to get better, you have to get out of your comfort zone. I did not find that convincing at the time.

I was terrified. Standing in front of an audience felt completely beyond me — I had no experience, no reference point for how to handle it, and no real choice in the matter. The nerves were very real. My knees were shaking and I was acutely aware of every awkward silence and stumbled sentence.

But I survived. And some time later there was another presentation, and then another, and gradually it got better. Not through any single insight — just through repetition. Each time, the fear was still there, but a little smaller than before.

## Passing it on

When I sense fear in someone — a developer reluctant to deploy a feature because something might break, a team avoiding a difficult conversation about requirements or design — I start by telling them the Chris story, if I have not already. Then I give my honest recommendation: the experience will almost certainly be less catastrophic than imagined, and the only way to find that out is to go through it. The fear probably will not disappear after a single attempt, but each time you get through something that frightened you, you accumulate evidence that you can — and there is a good chance it gets a little easier.

Some decide to go for it on their own — which is the whole point. If they are not ready for that, I offer options: I can support them, either as a backup if something goes wrong or by working through it together so they are not alone in it, or I can take it on entirely to relieve the pressure for now.
