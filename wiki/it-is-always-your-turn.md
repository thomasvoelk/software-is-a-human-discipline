# It Is Always Your Turn

**Summary**: Seth Godin's idea: there is no waiting for permission, no blaming the system. You can always do something.

**Sources**: Seth Godin. Personal practice. Connects directly to Christopher Avery's Responsibility Process.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-26

---

The idea is simple: it is always your turn. There is no situation in which you have no move available.

You can wait for the process to change. You can wait for the manager to decide. You can wait for the requirements to be clear. Or you can ask a question, start a conversation, write a prototype, flag a risk, propose an alternative. There is always something.

This sounds obvious. In practice, most people wait. They wait because acting feels risky, because ownership feels uncomfortable, because it is easier to say "the system won't let me."

## Connection to Responsibility Process

This is the practical application of Avery's [[responsibility-process]]. When you are at Responsibility — "I create my experience" — the next question is: what is your move? Godin answers: you always have one.

The combination is powerful: Avery gives you the mindset shift, Godin gives you the behavioural implication.

## In software teams

"We can't ship because X hasn't approved it" — whose turn is it? Yours. Go find X.

"The requirements are unclear" — whose turn is it? Yours. Go ask.

"Nobody told me about the architecture decision" — whose turn is it? Yours. Go learn.

This is not about blame. It is about agency. There is always a move.
