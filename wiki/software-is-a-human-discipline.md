# Software is a Human Discipline

**Summary**: The core thesis of this philosophy. The technical part of software development is the easy part. The hard part is people.

**Sources**: Personal experience, Kent Beck / Martin Fowler (https://youtu.be/ii_rLjQfjp0?t=3038), Gojko Adzic "Challenging Requirements".

**Last updated**: 2026-05-26

---

Software development in any non-trivial product is a collaborative undertaking. You work with customers, colleagues, architects, managers, junior developers — and all of them are human beings with different perspectives, needs, and fears.

I have practised this for a long time and I am convinced: **the technical part is the easy part.** The hard part is communication, trust, shared understanding, and the courage to ask the right questions.

This is not a common belief in our industry. Developers are hired for their technical skills and are often measured by output. But a brilliant solution designed in isolation is almost always inferior to a good solution built together. The collaboration is not overhead — it is the work.

Martin Fowler puts it well: "Talking to the computer is only part of the problem and it's actually in many ways the easiest. The key thing about computer programming is the communication between people."

Everything else in this wiki flows from this premise.

## Implications

- Your first job when joining a team or project is to become someone people can work *with*. See [[enchantment]] and [[nonviolent-communication]].
- You cannot build the right thing without understanding the actual problem. See [[challenge-the-solution]].
- Personal reactions — defensiveness, anger, blame — are the enemy of collaboration. See [[the-switch]].
- Mastery is not just technical. It includes domain knowledge and people skills. See [[learning-in-the-gaps]].
