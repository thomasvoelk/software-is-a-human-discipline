# Learning in the Gaps

**Summary**: Mastery in software development comes from what you do between features, not during them. The habit of learning in the gaps separates people who master programming from people who just do it.

**Sources**: Kent Beck and Martin Fowler discussion (https://youtu.be/ii_rLjQfjp0?t=3038). Personal practice and advice given over years.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-26

---

There will always be pressure to move immediately from one feature to the next. Faster, faster, faster. I have resisted this my whole career. Kent Beck articulates this better than I can:

> "The gaps between features is what's going to make you good and what you do with those gaps. [...] The habit of learning is what separates people who really master programming versus people who just do programming."

This is exactly how I got better. I did not just program what the customer wanted and move on. I used the gaps to experiment, explore, and learn things nobody asked me to learn.

Two examples that stuck with me: I taught myself TDD by reading about it and trying it, even though nobody in our company wrote any tests at all at the time. My early tests were bad. But I kept at it and got better. Another time I wrote a Java annotation processor just because I was curious about how it worked. It had little practical value — but I learned something new and expanded what I was capable of.

Nobody gave me permission to do any of that. I just did it.

## It is not just about code

Fowler adds the other half: talking to people who will benefit from the software is its own skill, and an important one. The gaps are also when you go deeper into the domain — understanding not just what to build, but *why*, and for whom.

> "Understanding a domain and being able to think to the point where you can actually bounce ideas off somebody as to what features to build can be really exciting. The key thing about computer programming is the communication between people."

Technical depth and domain depth grow in parallel. Neither is enough alone.

## The question of AI

In 2026, AI agents can write a lot of the code. The question of whether deep technical mastery matters in the same way it used to is genuinely open. What I am more confident about: domain understanding and people skills are *more* valuable, not less. The gaps are still where you build those.
