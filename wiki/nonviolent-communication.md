# Nonviolent Communication

**Summary**: Marshall Rosenberg's framework for communication. Most conflict is unmet needs, not bad people. Separating observations from evaluations is the foundation.

**Sources**: Marshall Rosenberg, "Nonviolent Communication". Personal practice in teams and mentoring.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-26

---

NVC is one of the first frameworks I show people when I mentor them. The core insight is this: most conflict between people is not really about the surface disagreement. It is about unmet needs. Once you see that, the conflict looks different and the path forward becomes clearer.

## The four components

1. **Observation** — what actually happened, without evaluation. "You were late to the meeting" not "you don't respect my time."
2. **Feelings** — what you feel as a result. Without blame or judgment.
3. **Needs** — the underlying need that is not being met.
4. **Request** — a concrete, actionable ask.

Most conversations in software teams skip straight to evaluation and demand. NVC slows that down.

## Two directions: expressing and giving empathy

NVC works in both directions. You can use the four components to express yourself — to say clearly what you observed, what you feel, what you need, and what you are asking for. But you can also use them to *give empathy*: listening to the other person for their feelings and needs, rather than just their words. "What do they actually need right now?" is a very different question than "what are they saying?"

In practice, giving empathy is often the more powerful move. When someone pushes back hard or seems difficult, asking yourself what need they might be expressing changes how you receive them. See also the [[curiosity-over-anger]] page.

## Why it matters in software teams

Code reviews. Architecture debates. Retrospectives. Missed deadlines. These are all situations where people have unmet needs — for recognition, for safety, for trust, for clarity — and those needs come out sideways as criticism, defensiveness, or silence.

I have practised NVC in team settings for a long time. It does not make disagreement disappear. It makes it legible.

## Giraffe ears and the amygdala

Rosenberg uses the giraffe as NVC's symbol — it has the largest heart of any land animal. Listening with "giraffe ears" means hearing what someone says not as criticism or attack, but as an expression of their feelings and needs.

This is a different intervention point than the Rainsberger [[curiosity-over-anger]] switch. The switch kicks in *after* the threat response fires — you feel the anger rising and you flip. Giraffe ears work earlier — you reframe the incoming message as a need before you have fully processed it as a threat. If you hear "this code is a mess" through giraffe ears, you hear someone expressing frustration and a need for clarity — not an attack on your identity. Whether this fully prevents the physiological reaction or just shortens it, I cannot say. But in practice it feels like a different, calmer place to receive difficult messages from.

Two different points of intervention on the same chain. Both are worth developing.

## Connection to other frameworks

The anger that Rainsberger's [[curiosity-over-anger]] switch addresses is often — at the root — an unmet need. NVC gives you the vocabulary to name it. The [[responsibility-process]] asks "what can I do?" — NVC helps you understand what you actually need before you act.
