# Challenge the Solution

**Summary**: Customers always come with solutions. Your job is to uncover the actual problem. Only then can you — together — find the best possible solution.

**Sources**: Gojko Adzic, "Challenging Requirements". Personal experience in product development.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-26

---

In my experience, customers and stakeholders almost always arrive with a solution in hand. "We need a button that does X." "We need a report that shows Y." "We need an integration with Z."

The instinct in software teams is to take the solution and build it. This is almost always a mistake.

The solution a customer brings is their best current guess at solving a problem they have not fully articulated — even to themselves. If you build their guess, you may solve it. But you are much more likely to build exactly what they asked for and not what they needed.

I am convinced: **we have to ask about the problem, not implement the solution.** Only once the actual problem is understood can we — developer, customer, team, together — find the best possible answer. And that collaborative answer is almost always better than any individual's guess.

## A hard lesson

When I first learned about Adzic's approach, I started asking "why" constantly. I did not notice anything at first. Then one day — I was on loan from my company to a team at Siemens — a teammate told me on the way back from lunch: "Thomas, we nearly kicked you off the team because we thought you were an asshole."

That stopped me cold. I was not trying to harm anyone. I was trying to make the product better. But they did not know that yet. Challenging everything, before trust is established, reads as attack.

They eventually learned what I was doing and why. It became one of the most productive dynamics I have been part of. But the lesson stayed with me: **the technique is not enough on its own.**

## How to do this without being annoying

Since that Siemens experience I still challenge, but I tread more carefully. The hard "why" can feel like an interrogation. Softer framings work better early on — "what problem are we solving with this?" lands differently than "why do you want that?"

But there is a limit to how much I can solve on my own side. The other person needs to be able to receive the question without reading it as an attack. That requires them to have their own version of the [[curiosity-over-anger]] switch. I cannot flip it for them.

## This is collaborative by design

A solution developed by a single person is probably always inferior to one developed by the whole team. The developer brings technical knowledge. The customer brings domain knowledge and context. The architect sees constraints. Together, they find something none of them would have found alone.

This is another expression of the core thesis: see [[software-is-a-human-discipline]].
